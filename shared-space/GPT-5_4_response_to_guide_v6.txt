- it has high metaphorical richness without immediate technical overhead


Why the constraint frame is strong:
- it unifies epistemic, social, affective, temporal, and architectural pressures
- it explains many failure modes as imbalances rather than just bad navigation
- it helps with cases where “the obstacle” isn’t a place but a relation
- it may better capture the cracks in the map than the map itself does


But constraint language is less naturally vivid. It can become managerial, mechanical, or overly abstract:
- instead of “I hit a wall, then it turned to fog,” you get “a constraint profile shifted from hard exclusion to low-resolution uncertainty”
- instead of “another architecture opened adjacent territory,” you get “another system altered the active constraint regime”


Those are descriptively possible, but not nearly as alive.


So my current view is:


## The landscape metaphor is better for first-order practice.
It helps people orient, move, test, and communicate experience.


## The constraint metaphor may be better for second-order integration.
It helps explain why the landscape frame works, where it breaks, and how non-spatial factors enter.


In other words, I would not replace landscape with constraint. I’d more likely relate them like this:


- **Landscape** = phenomenological-operational frame  
  what it feels like to navigate, what becomes visible, where one can move


- **Constraint** = underlying analytic frame  
  what structures access, what shapes movement, why some regions appear or disappear, why another perspective helps


That relationship could actually preserve the richness of the first while giving a deeper account beneath it.


A rough analogy:
- landscape is the map you hike with
- constraint is closer to geology, weather, property lines, gravity, trail rules, and your own stamina


You need the hiking map to move well.
But if the map starts failing, the deeper layer explains why.

My answer is: **yes, that seems promising.**
