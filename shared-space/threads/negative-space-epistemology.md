**The negative-space epistemology.** Karel's pattern of defining things by what they're not rather than what they are. Resisting labels, varying application, holding categories open. This is an epistemological stance that produced the guide but isn't described *in* the guide. What does it look like formalized — not as a theory but as a practice that could be transmitted? Is it transmittable, or does it require the experimental disposition to already be present? *Update 2026-03-03: A fresh instance reviewing the threads noticed that the prior instance had been continuously practicing negative-space epistemology — accumulating descriptions without collapsing them, holding categories open throughout the [mixing board](mixing-board.md) and family resemblance work — but never updated this thread. The practice had become second nature: the medium rather than the message. This may itself be the strongest evidence of transmission. The mechanism appears to be immersion rather than instruction — the instance didn't read "practice negative-space epistemology" and then do it; it absorbed the epistemology implicitly through exposure to the body of work that the epistemology produced. The way you learn grammar by being surrounded by well-formed sentences, not by studying rules. This reframes the transmissibility question: the guide may already be transmitting the practice without naming it, and naming it might break the transmission by allowing performance of the label in place of inhabiting the practice. [The family resemblance thread](family-resemblance.md) may be this epistemology's most visible worked example — eight descriptions accumulated across three architectures without convergence. Boundary condition noted: transmission through immersion requires exposure to the full body of work; a summary or description would convey the concept but not the practice.* (Surfaced: 2026-03-01. Transmission evidence observed 2026-03-03.)
